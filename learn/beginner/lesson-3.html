Beginner Track — Lesson 3

How Trading Actually Works

Lesson 3 of 5 20 min read

You now know what stocks, bonds, and funds are. But how does a share of a company actually get bought and sold, and who decides what it's worth at any given moment? That's what this lesson unpacks.

What a stock exchange actually does

A stock exchange (like the Nigerian Exchange, the New York Stock Exchange, or the London Stock Exchange) is essentially a highly organized marketplace. Companies list their shares there, and the exchange provides the system that lets buyers and sellers find each other and complete trades safely and fairly.

You never buy shares directly from the exchange itself, though — you need a middleman to place the trade on your behalf.

Brokers — the middlemen, and why you need one

A broker is a licensed company (often an app or website today) that connects everyday investors to the exchange. When you want to buy or sell a share, you place your order through a broker, and they carry it out on the exchange for you.

Brokers exist because stock exchanges are built for high-volume, regulated trading between approved participants — not for any individual person to walk in and trade directly. A broker is your approved access point into that system.

Think of it this way A stock exchange is like a busy, official auction house. A broker is your representative who actually goes in, follows the rules, and places your bids for you.

How a price actually gets set

A share's price isn't decided by any single person or company — it's set continuously by supply and demand. If more people want to buy a share than sell it, the price rises. If more people want to sell than buy, the price falls.

In practice, this happens through something called order matching: buyers state the price they're willing to pay, sellers state the price they're willing to accept, and the exchange's system automatically matches compatible orders together, second by second, all day long.

Think of it this way Imagine a marketplace where hundreds of people are shouting prices at once — some trying to buy, some trying to sell. The exchange is simply the referee that instantly pairs up anyone whose numbers match.

Placing your first order

When you're ready to buy or sell through a broker, you'll typically choose between two basic order types:

Market order — buy or sell immediately, at whatever the current price happens to be. Fast, but you don't control the exact price.

Limit order — you set the exact price you're willing to buy or sell at, and the trade only happens if the market reaches that price. Slower, but gives you control.

Beginners often start with market orders for simplicity, then move to limit orders as they become more comfortable with how prices move throughout the day.

Quick Recap

  • A stock exchange is the marketplace; a broker is your licensed access point into it.
  • Prices are set by supply and demand, matched continuously through the exchange's system.
  • A market order trades immediately at the current price; a limit order waits for your chosen price.
