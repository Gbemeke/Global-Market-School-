Beginner Track — Lesson 2

Markets 101

Lesson 2 of 5 18 min read

In Lesson 1, we saw why people invest instead of just saving. Now let's look at the three things people actually invest in — stocks, bonds, and funds — and what a "market" really is.

What a market actually is

When people say "the market," they don't mean a single building or website. A market is simply any place — physical or digital — where buyers and sellers come together to trade something at an agreed price. A financial market works exactly the same way, except what's being traded is ownership, debt, or a basket of both.

Stocks — owning a small piece of a company

A stock (also called a share) represents one small piece of ownership in a company. If a company issues 1,000,000 shares and you own 1,000 of them, you own 0.1% of that company.

Why would a company let strangers own pieces of it? Because selling shares is how companies raise money to grow — build factories, hire people, expand into new markets — without having to borrow it. In return, if the company grows and becomes more valuable, the value of your shares grows too. If it struggles, your shares can lose value as well.

Think of it this way Buying a share is like buying one slice of a pizza the company baked. If the pizza gets bigger (the company grows), your slice is worth more — even though you didn't do any of the baking yourself.

Bonds — lending money and earning interest

A bond works completely differently from a stock. Instead of buying ownership, you're lending money — to a company or a government — for a fixed period of time. In exchange, they promise to pay you back the full amount later, plus regular interest payments along the way.

Bonds are generally considered safer than stocks, because you're owed a specific amount back, rather than depending on how well a company performs. But safer usually also means the potential growth is smaller.

Funds — buying many things at once

A fund pools money from many investors and uses it to buy a whole collection of stocks, bonds, or both, in one single purchase. Instead of picking one company's stock and hoping it does well, a fund spreads your money across dozens or hundreds of them at once.

Think of it this way Buying one stock is like betting on one horse in a race. Buying a fund is like owning a small piece of every horse — if one stumbles, the others can still carry your overall result.

We'll cover exactly why that spreading-out matters — it's called diversification — in more depth in Lesson 4. For now, the key idea is simply this: stocks, bonds, and funds are the three basic building blocks almost every investor uses, in different combinations, depending on how much risk they're comfortable with.

Three building blocks, three different ways to put your money to work.

Quick Recap

  • A market is any place buyers and sellers trade at an agreed price — a financial market trades ownership and debt.
  • A stock is a small piece of ownership in a company; its value rises and falls with the company.
  • A bond is a loan you give, in exchange for your money back plus interest.
  • A fund pools money to buy many stocks and/or bonds at once, spreading out the risk.
