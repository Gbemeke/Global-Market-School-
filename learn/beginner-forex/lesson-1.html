Beginner Forex Track — Lesson 1

What Is Forex?

Lesson 1 of 5 15 min read

Alongside stocks and bonds, there's an entirely different market — the largest financial market in the world, in fact — built around trading one country's currency for another. This is the forex market.

Why currencies need to be traded at all

If a Nigerian company wants to buy machinery from Germany, it needs euros, not naira. If a Kenyan business exports coffee to the United States, it gets paid in dollars, but needs shillings to pay its local staff. Every single day, businesses, travelers, and governments need to exchange one currency for another — and the forex market (short for "foreign exchange") is where that happens.

Unlike a stock exchange with one central building or system, forex trading happens electronically, between banks, brokers, and traders worldwide, essentially 24 hours a day during the business week.

Currency pairs — always two at once

You can never buy a currency by itself — you're always exchanging one currency for another, at the same time. This is why forex is always quoted as a currency pair, like USD/NGN (US Dollar to Nigerian Naira) or EUR/USD (Euro to US Dollar).

The first currency listed is called the base currency; the second is the quote currency. The pair's price tells you how much of the quote currency it takes to buy one unit of the base currency.

USD/NGN at 1,600 means one US Dollar buys 1,600 Nigerian Naira.

Think of it this way A currency pair is like a seesaw. If the base currency strengthens, it takes more of the quote currency to balance it — the number goes up. If the base weakens, the number goes down.

What makes forex different from stocks

When you buy a stock, you're betting on one company's performance. When you trade forex, you're essentially betting on the relative strength of one economy against another — because a currency's value reflects how the rest of the world views that country's economic health, trade, and stability.

This means forex prices are driven heavily by the same kind of economic forces we touched on in Beginner Lesson 5 — interest rates, inflation, and broader economic news — just applied to comparing two countries against each other, rather than one company against its industry.

Quick Recap

  • Forex is the market where currencies are traded, needed by businesses, travelers, and governments daily.
  • Currencies are always quoted in pairs — a base currency and a quote currency.
  • Forex prices reflect the relative economic strength of two countries against each other.
